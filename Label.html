The Label

Our insperation

[1] As explained in the motivations page, the goal of our label is to give accurate and clear data to shoppers, so they can make easy comparisons between substitutional products on their environmental impact. With how busy the world has become, it can’t be expected for people to do research on every product they could potentially buy. Companies know this and use this asymmetric information to mislead shoppers. We are here to stop this. Our labelling system provides accurate data on relevant pollutants on all products that a shopper would buy. Consistent labelling and the use of our ‘traffic light system’ would allow customers to understand and compare easily, even when in a rush. Ensuring that they can reach their own environmental goals whilst keeping up with the rest of their lives. The traffic light system, based on the same system used on food products, has been proven to be more effective at influencing shopper choices than alternatives in black or white, or those without data. A meta analysis showed that with the traffic light system, shoppers were 29.36% more likely to go for the healthier option. Due to our system being similar to the one used with food products the barrier to understanding should be significantly lower, as shoppers are familiar with the system and therefore more likely to engage with it.

How to interperet our label

On the label itself, it would consist of up to 5 categories on the petals of a flower. These would feature the pollutant, the mass of that pollutant produced per unit of product (eg per tablet in a bag of dishwasher tablets) and colours representative of the ranking of this value compared to other products in its group. An overall rank of the products environmental impact will be placed in the center and the colour of the centre and stem will represent where it ranks compared with other products.

How Products are grouped

The groups in which these products would be ranked are decided as follows: products that serve the same purpose when used. For example, all toothpastes would be in the same group, even if one states its purpose is for ‘whitening’ or for ‘rebuilding enamel’, however mouthwash would not be included in the group as it does not replace toothpaste and the act of brushing the teeth must still be done. In addition groupings would include all products of that group sold in the UK. This means that even if your local supermarket does not include all the products within their group, the rankings on the products that are there will include them. Although products within the same group have similar compositions, products of different groups may have vastly different contents. These contents may have different methods of raw material extraction, refining and production processes which can lead to different pollutants being more relevant to different product groups. The categories that we have chosen to use are as follows: Greenhouse gases (using equivalent CO2 mass for non-CO2 gases) Hazardous waste ( only includes waste not correctly managed) Recyclability of the product Overall Company impact

How we decided what to track

When choosing which pollutants to choose from we looked at the following categories: Prevalence in Manufacturing Notoriety to Public Acute Harm to Environment Long-term Harm to Planet How well researched Direct harm to humans Reach of effects Quickness to Reduce Cost to Reduce Traceability/Transparency From this we decided that Greenhouse gases are essential for our labelling system, they are extremely prevalent in the conversation of environmental conservation, being the leading cause of climate change. They are also the main pollutant people associate with the idea of being green. Within the category of Hazardous waste there is the sub-group of toxic gas emissions. In which we have included molecules such as: nitrous oxides, sulfur oxides and VOCs (Volatile organic compounds). The other category is hazardous soluble materials such as heavy metals, PFA’s and microplastics. These compounds are capable of leeching into plants and animals resulting in build-up of toxic material in the environment which can damage ecosystems. In addition these compounds commonly result in these pollutants entering our food-chains which can result in health complications. As previously mentioned. Different product groups would likely have different pollutants that are most relevant in their production and therefore on the label only the two most relevant pollutants from this category would be featured on the label. For the category of ‘recyclability’ would use the UKs Recyclability Assessment methodology (RAM) and would be represented on the packaging as either green, amber or Red. For the ‘Overall company impact’ this would include environmental projects in which the company is a part of. This value would be shown on the packaging as an A-F scale.
